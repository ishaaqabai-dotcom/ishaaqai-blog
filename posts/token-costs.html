Why Every Ops Leader Needs to Understand Token Costs in 2026

Ishaaq Aba Bagnan• December 18, 2025• 6 min read
📈

AI is not free. This should be obvious, yet it is a fact that most operations leaders are still treating as someone else's problem. As LLMs get embedded into customer support queues, internal knowledge bases, document processing pipelines, and reporting workflows, the cost of running those systems is no longer a line item that only engineers see. It is an operational expense that belongs on the ops leader's desk alongside headcount and tooling budgets.

Token costs are the unit economics of AI. Understanding them does not require a technical background. It requires the same instinct you apply to any cost driver in your operation: know what you are buying, know what drives the bill up, and know how to make the spend deliberate.

What a Token Actually Is

Language models do not read words the way humans do. They process chunks of text called tokens. A token is roughly four characters of English text, which means a typical word is one to two tokens. A paragraph is around 100 tokens. A full-length report might be 2,000 tokens or more.

Every time you send text to a language model and receive a response, you are consuming tokens on both sides. The input -- your prompt, the context you provide, any documents you attach -- costs input tokens. The response costs output tokens. Most models price these separately, with output typically costing two to four times more than input per token.

"Token costs are the unit economics of AI. Every team embedding LLMs into workflows is running a business inside a business -- and most of them have no idea what their margins look like."

Why This Is an Ops Problem, Not an Engineering Problem

Engineers set up the pipelines. But the decisions that drive token consumption are operational decisions: how much context to pass, how frequently to run a process, whether to summarise or send the full document, how many steps a workflow has. These are not code decisions. They are workflow design decisions.

An operations team that decides to run a daily AI summary of every customer interaction across 500 accounts has made a cost decision whether they know it or not. At typical API pricing, that kind of workflow can cost hundreds of dollars per month before any optimisation. Multiply that by the number of AI-assisted workflows across a mid-size operation and the bill becomes significant quickly.

The Three Cost Drivers to Track

  • Context length: The more text you send with every request, the higher the cost. Passing a full 20-page document to answer a simple question is expensive. Passing only the relevant section is not.
  • Call frequency: How often does this workflow run? Hourly checks cost 24 times more per day than daily checks. Match frequency to actual business need.
  • Output verbosity: Long responses cost more than short ones. If your workflow only needs a classification or a yes/no answer, prompt for exactly that -- not a paragraph.

A Practical Framework for Cost-Aware AI Operations

You do not need to monitor every API call. You need a framework for making cost-aware decisions at the workflow design stage, before the bill arrives.

Step 1: Classify your AI workflows by cost profile

Before worrying about individual token counts, categorise each AI-assisted workflow as low, medium, or high cost. Low-cost workflows run infrequently with short inputs and outputs. High-cost workflows run constantly, process large documents, or generate lengthy responses. This simple classification tells you where to focus attention.

Step 2: Set a token budget for each workflow class

Work with your engineering team to define expected token ranges per run for each workflow class. A customer triage workflow should cost X tokens per ticket. A document summariser should cost Y tokens per document. When actuals exceed the budget, that is a signal to investigate -- not a crisis, but a flag.

Example token budget targets: Customer query classification: 150-300 tokens/request Daily ops summary (team): 800-1,200 tokens/run Document summariser (10-page): 2,000-3,500 tokens/doc Full report generation: 4,000-8,000 tokens/run

Step 3: Review the top 20% of spenders monthly

Pareto applies to token costs as reliably as it applies to everything else. A small number of workflows will account for the majority of your AI spend. Review those monthly. Ask whether the frequency is justified, whether the context can be trimmed, and whether the output length is necessary.

The Strategic Angle: Cost as a Moat

There is a competitive dimension here that goes beyond keeping costs down. Operations teams that build cost discipline into their AI workflows from the start will have a structural advantage over those that do not. As AI becomes a commodity capability, the differentiator will not be who uses AI -- it will be who uses it most efficiently.

The teams that will win are the ones building lean, targeted AI workflows with clear cost-per-outcome metrics. Not the ones running the most agents, but the ones running the right agents at the right cost to deliver measurable value.

Token costs are not a tax on progress. They are a signal. Pay attention to them now, before the bill demands your attention for you.
