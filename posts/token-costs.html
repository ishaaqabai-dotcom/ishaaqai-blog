Why Every Ops Leader Needs to Understand Token Costs in 2026

Ishaaq Aba Bagnan• Dec 18, 2025• 6 min read
📊

When I talk to operations leaders about their AI deployments, the conversation almost always focuses on capability: what the model can do, how accurate it is, what workflows it can automate. Almost nobody brings up costs until they get their first surprise invoice. By then, the decisions that drove those costs are already embedded in the architecture, and reversing them is expensive.

Token costs are not an engineering problem. They are an operational problem. And operations leaders need to understand them with the same fluency they apply to headcount costs, software licence costs, and infrastructure costs. Here is the foundation.

What Tokens Are and Why They Matter

A token is roughly four characters of text, or about three-quarters of a word. Everything processed by a large language model -- your instructions, the documents you feed it, its outputs, the conversation history -- is measured and charged in tokens. Most frontier models charge separately for input tokens (what you send in) and output tokens (what the model generates), with output tokens typically costing two to four times as much as input tokens.

This asymmetry has direct operational implications. A system that asks a model to produce long, verbose outputs is significantly more expensive than one that constrains outputs to what is strictly necessary. A retrieval-augmented generation system that dumps entire documents into context is far more expensive per query than one that retrieves and passes only the relevant passages. These are design choices, and they have cost consequences that compound at scale.

"Token costs are not visible until they are large. By the time they are large, the architecture that is driving them is usually deeply embedded."

The Operational Cost Model

Thinking about AI costs operationally means thinking in terms of cost per transaction rather than cost per month. If your customer support AI handles 10,000 conversations per month at an average of 2,000 input tokens and 500 output tokens per conversation, you can calculate your monthly spend precisely and forecast it as volume scales. That is operational thinking.

It also means understanding which workflows are cost-sensitive and which are not. A research summarisation task that runs once a week and saves three hours of analyst time is cost-insensitive -- even at a high per-run cost, the ROI is obvious. A customer-facing system that runs thousands of times per day at high token volumes is cost-sensitive -- even small inefficiencies in context management compound into significant monthly spend.

The Four Levers Operations Leaders Should Know

There are four practical levers for managing token costs without degrading output quality. First, context management: be precise about what information you include in prompts. The marginal token is cheap in isolation but expensive at scale. Second, output constraint: specify the format and length of desired outputs explicitly. A model that defaults to verbose prose will spend three times the tokens of one constrained to structured, concise responses. Third, model selection: frontier models are not always the right choice. A smaller, cheaper model running with a well-crafted prompt will outperform a frontier model with a poorly designed one on many operational tasks. Fourth, caching: for prompts with stable system instructions, prompt caching can reduce costs significantly by avoiding re-processing of unchanged content.

Making the Business Case

The conversation I recommend operations leaders have with their finance counterparts is not "AI costs money" but "here is the cost per transaction, here is the volume, here is the cost per outcome, and here is how it compares to the cost of the human workflow it replaces or augments." That framing turns an open-ended AI budget line into a unit economics conversation, which is a conversation operations leaders know how to have.

AI is not free, and the costs are not trivial at scale. But they are predictable, manageable, and almost always favourable when measured against the alternative. The leaders who understand this now will build better systems and make better investment decisions than those who treat token costs as an afterthought.
