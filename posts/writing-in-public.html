The Compounding Returns of Writing in Public Every Week

Ishaaq Aba Bagnan• Jan 15, 2026• 4 min read
🌟

I want to be careful not to over-romanticise writing in public, because most of the discourse around it does exactly that. The pitch usually sounds like this: write consistently, build an audience, unlock opportunities. That is not wrong. But it is incomplete in ways that cause a lot of people to start writing in public for the wrong reasons, get disappointed when the obvious returns do not materialise quickly, and quietly stop.

The real value of writing in public is more subtle and more durable than an audience metric. It is the compounding effect that happens inside your own thinking over months and years of regular output. The audience, if it comes, is a downstream consequence of that internal compounding. It is not the mechanism.

Writing Clarifies Thinking -- but Only If You Let It

There is a version of writing in public that does not improve your thinking at all. It is the version where you write summaries of things you have already read, share takes you already held, and produce content that confirms your existing views for an audience that shares those views. That kind of writing is comfortable. It is also intellectually inert.

The writing that compounds is the writing that exposes you to friction. Writing an argument clearly enough to publish it reveals the gaps in the argument. Writing a prediction publicly creates accountability. Writing about a mistake you made requires you to actually understand what went wrong, not just feel bad about it. That friction is the mechanism. Without it, writing in public is performance, not practice.

"You do not write to share what you think. You write to discover what you think. The sharing is secondary."

The Compounding That Actually Happens

After two years of weekly writing, here is what I can point to as real compounding returns. My ability to structure an argument has measurably improved -- I can feel it in how I prepare for presentations and how I frame decisions for my team. My thinking has a searchable record: when I encounter a problem I have thought about before, I can find what I wrote rather than re-deriving it from scratch. And the body of work has become a filter: people who reach out to me after reading several pieces tend to be people I want to talk to, because they have already self-selected based on what I actually think.

None of these returns show up in follower counts or engagement metrics. All of them are genuinely valuable. And all of them emerged from the practice, not from optimising for the platform.

What to Do With the Slow Periods

There will be extended periods where you publish consistently and nothing seems to happen. The numbers stay flat. The posts land quietly. Nobody shares or responds. This is normal. It is also where most people stop.

The advice I keep coming back to for the slow periods is to change the metric you are tracking. Instead of watching follower counts or impression numbers, track whether you are thinking more clearly than you were three months ago. Track whether you find it easier to articulate a complex idea in a short post than you did six months ago. Track whether your writing is becoming more specific, more precise, more honest over time.

Those are the metrics that predict long-term value. The platform metrics tend to follow them eventually -- but even if they never do, the internal compounding is worth the practice.
