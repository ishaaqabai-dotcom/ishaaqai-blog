How I Built a 5-Agent Automation in 48 Hours

Ishaaq Aba Bagnan• Feb 28, 2026• 5 min read
🔨

Last month I set myself a constraint: design and deploy a working five-agent automation system in 48 hours. No shortcuts, no pre-built templates. Just a goal, a whiteboard, and a weekend. Here is exactly what I built and what I learned along the way.

The use case was content operations -- specifically the pipeline from raw topic idea to published post with share-ready social copy. It sounds simple. It is not. There are at least a dozen handoffs, judgment calls, and format transforms involved. I wanted agents to handle all of them.

Hour 0-8: Designing the System Before Touching Code

The biggest mistake people make when building multi-agent systems is jumping straight into implementation. The second biggest mistake is designing the agents first instead of the workflow. I did neither. I started with the output: what does a finished, published post with share copy look like? Then I worked backwards.

By hour three I had a clear map: Agent 1 researches the topic and produces a structured brief. Agent 2 writes a first draft from that brief. Agent 3 reviews the draft against a style guide and returns edits. Agent 4 formats the final article into HTML. Agent 5 generates platform-specific social copy from the published piece. Five agents. Five distinct responsibilities. Clean handoffs between each.

"Design the workflow first. Then design the agents. Most people do it backwards and wonder why their system falls apart at the seams."

Hour 8-24: Building and Wiring

The build phase was faster than I expected -- and harder than I expected, but in different places. The individual agents were straightforward to configure. The hard part was the data contracts between them. What exactly does Agent 1 pass to Agent 2? In what format? What happens if Agent 3 sends back a rejection rather than edits?

I spent most of this phase writing what I call "interface specs" -- short documents describing what each agent receives, what it must return, and what error states it can emit. This turned out to be the highest-leverage work of the entire 48 hours. Every time something broke in testing, I could trace it directly back to a missing or ambiguous interface spec.

By hour 24, three of the five agents were working end-to-end. Agent 4 (the HTML formatter) kept hallucinating CSS that did not exist in my stylesheet. Agent 5 (social copy) was writing LinkedIn posts that sounded like press releases. Both were fixable with better prompts and clearer output constraints.

Hour 24-48: Testing, Fixing, Shipping

I ran the full pipeline twelve times over the final 24 hours, each time with a different topic. The failure rate started at around 40% -- meaning at least one agent in the chain produced output that required human intervention. By the end, I had it down to under 10%, and most of those failures were in Agent 3, the reviewer, which occasionally got overly aggressive with edits and broke the voice of the piece.

The lesson there: reviewer agents need tighter constraints than writer agents. Give them a rubric, not open-ended latitude.

What I Would Do Differently

Three things. First, I would write the interface specs before touching any agent configuration -- even before mapping the workflow. Second, I would build a shared logging layer from the start rather than bolting it on at the end. Third, I would plan for human-in-the-loop checkpoints explicitly rather than assuming I could remove them later.

The 48-hour constraint was artificial, but it was useful. It forced ruthless prioritisation and prevented me from over-engineering early. The system I shipped is not perfect, but it runs. And running imperfectly beats designing perfectly every time.
