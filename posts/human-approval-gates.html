The Bottleneck Nobody Talks About: Human Approval Gates

Ishaaq Aba Bagnan• Feb 20, 2026• 6 min read
⚙️

There is a bottleneck in most operations teams that nobody talks about in retrospect reviews, nobody flags in process audits, and nobody puts on a roadmap to fix. It sits between every automated step and every human decision-maker, and it is responsible for more velocity loss than almost any technical limitation. It is the human approval gate.

I am not anti-human oversight. Quite the opposite. But I have spent enough time mapping operational workflows to know that most approval gates are not there because someone decided they needed to be. They are there because nobody decided they did not need to be. That is a different problem entirely.

What an Approval Gate Actually Costs

The obvious cost is time. A task that takes 10 minutes to execute sits in a queue waiting for a 30-second approval that takes 48 hours to arrive because the approver is in back-to-back meetings on Monday and travelling on Tuesday. You have not added oversight to your process. You have added a 48-hour delay to your 10-minute process.

The less obvious cost is context-switching. Every time a task reaches a human gate, that human must re-load context: what is this, why does it need my input, what are the options, what are the downstream implications. That cognitive overhead is real, and it compounds across the dozens of small approvals that land in a manager's inbox every week.

"Most approval gates are not there because someone decided they were needed. They are there because nobody decided they were not."

Three Types of Approval Gate -- and Which to Kill

Not all approval gates are equal. Type one is the compliance gate: legally or contractually required human sign-off before an action is taken. Do not touch these. They exist for good reasons and the risk of removing them far outweighs any velocity gain.

Type two is the quality gate: a human reviews output before it goes further downstream because the consequences of bad output are high. These are worth examining carefully. In many cases, a well-configured automated review step -- comparing output against a rubric, checking for anomalies, flagging only the exceptions -- can replace a blanket human review with targeted human review of the 5% that actually needs it.

Type three is the legacy gate: an approval step that was added at some point in the past for a reason that no longer exists, and nobody has removed it because it has become invisible infrastructure. These are your quick wins. Audit your workflows for approval steps that are over six months old and ask the approver what the last three things they rejected were. If the answer is "nothing recently" or "I cannot remember," that gate is probably ready to go.

Building the Right Escalation Model Instead

The goal is not to remove human judgment from operations. The goal is to make human judgment scarce, valuable, and applied only where it actually matters. That means designing systems that run autonomously by default and escalate to humans only when a defined threshold is crossed -- a confidence score drops below a level, a value exceeds a limit, or a novel situation is detected that the system has not seen before.

This is a fundamentally different design pattern from the traditional approval model. Instead of "nothing moves without a human sign-off," you get "everything moves unless a human needs to intervene." That inversion, when implemented correctly, preserves oversight without sacrificing speed. The human is still in the loop. They are just in the right part of the loop.

Start by mapping every approval step in one critical workflow. Label each one as compliance, quality, or legacy. Remove the legacy ones. Automate the quality ones where the risk is low. Protect the compliance ones. Then measure the time-to-completion before and after. The number will surprise you.
