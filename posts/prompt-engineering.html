Prompt Engineering Is a Skill. Here Is How to Actually Get Good at It.

Ishaaq Aba Bagnan• January 8, 2026• 9 min read
🤖

The term prompt engineering has attracted both too much reverence and too much dismissal. On one side, people selling courses treat it as an arcane craft requiring months of study. On the other, engineers who work directly with model internals wave it away as "just writing sentences." Both camps are wrong, and the gap between them is where the practical skill actually lives.

Prompt engineering is a skill in the same sense that writing clearly is a skill -- learnable, improvable with practice, and consequential enough to matter. Here is a structured way to actually develop it.

Understand What You Are Actually Doing

When you write a prompt, you are not giving instructions to a system that understands instructions the way a human does. You are constructing a context that makes the desired output the statistically most likely continuation. This distinction matters because it changes how you think about failure.

When a prompt produces a bad output, the question is not "why didn't it follow my instructions?" The question is "what in the context I constructed made this output likely?" That reframe -- from compliance failure to context failure -- is the foundational mental model of good prompt engineering.

"A prompt is not a command. It is a context that makes your desired output the path of least resistance for the model."

The Five Levers of Prompt Quality

1. Role and Persona

Establishing a role at the start of a prompt activates relevant knowledge and sets stylistic expectations. "You are a senior operations consultant" produces different outputs than "You are a friendly assistant" for the same task. The role primes the model's internal representation of who is writing.

2. Task Specification

Vague tasks produce vague outputs. "Write about AI" produces something generic. "Write a 400-word opinion piece arguing that most organisations are over-investing in AI tools and under-investing in AI literacy, targeted at operations leaders in the UAE" produces something specific and usable. Every degree of specificity in the task reduces the range of acceptable outputs and increases the probability that the model produces the one you want.

3. Format Instructions

Tell the model exactly what the output should look like. Headers or no headers? Bullet points or prose? How long? What tone? What reading level? These constraints are not limitations -- they are signals that help the model produce exactly what you need rather than its best guess at what might be wanted.

BAD: "Summarise this report." GOOD: "Summarise this report in exactly 5 bullet points. Each bullet must be one sentence. Focus only on findings relevant to cost reduction. Use direct, non-hedged language."

4. Examples

Few-shot examples are the single highest-leverage prompt engineering technique for most practical applications. Showing the model two or three examples of input-output pairs that match your desired format is almost always more effective than describing the format in words. The model learns from demonstration faster than from instruction.

5. Constraints and Guardrails

Explicitly state what you do not want. "Do not use bullet points." "Do not mention competitors." "Do not hedge with phrases like 'it depends'." Negative constraints are often more effective than their positive equivalents because they directly eliminate the failure modes you have already observed.

The Iteration Loop

The fastest way to improve at prompting is to treat every suboptimal output as a diagnosis problem rather than a failure. When the output is wrong, ask: which of the five levers is responsible? Is the role wrong? Is the task underspecified? Is the format ambiguous? Is there a missing constraint? Fix one lever at a time and observe the effect. This deliberate, diagnostic iteration builds intuition far faster than trial and error.

  • Keep a personal library of prompts that work. Reuse and adapt them.
  • Version your prompts. Note what changed and what improved.
  • Test the same prompt across different models. The variations reveal model-specific behaviours worth knowing.

Prompt engineering is not a destination. It is a practice that improves every time you pay deliberate attention to why an output was or was not what you needed. Start paying that attention and the skill develops faster than you expect.
