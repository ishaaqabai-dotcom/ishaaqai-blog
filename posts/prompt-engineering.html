Prompt Engineering Is a Skill. Here Is How to Actually Get Good at It.

Ishaaq Aba Bagnan• Jan 8, 2026• 9 min read
🤖

Most people's understanding of prompt engineering stops at "write better instructions." That is like saying the key to programming is "write better code." It is true, technically, but it tells you nothing useful about how to actually do it. Prompt engineering is a genuine technical skill with learnable components, and it is misunderstood in ways that hold back the people who need it most.

Let me be specific about what I mean by the skill. Prompt engineering, done well, requires an understanding of how language models process context, where they are prone to failure, how their outputs change with temperature and formatting, and how to structure multi-step interactions so each step sets up the next. That is a non-trivial body of knowledge. Here is how to actually build it.

Understand the Context Window First

Before you worry about the wording of your prompt, understand the container it lives in. A language model processes everything in its context window simultaneously -- your instructions, any examples you provide, any retrieved documents, the conversation history, and its own prior outputs. The relative position and salience of each element matters.

In practice, this means two things. First, the most important instructions belong at the beginning and at the end of your prompt -- recency and primacy both have measurable effects on model behaviour. Second, long, dense context windows dilute attention. If you pack 10,000 tokens of background documents into a context and then ask a specific question about a specific detail, the model may miss it. Structure your context so the relevant information is proximate to the question.

"Prompt engineering is not about magic words. It is about understanding that you are communicating with a system that has specific, learnable behaviours -- and learning them."

Work Backwards from Failure Modes

The fastest way to improve your prompting is to study how and why your current prompts fail. Not just "the output was wrong" but specifically: was the output wrong because the model misunderstood the task, because it lacked necessary information, because it was overconstrained by the format you specified, or because it made a plausible but incorrect inference?

Each failure type has a different fix. Misunderstood task: add a concrete example of the desired output. Missing information: ensure all necessary context is in the prompt or retrievable by the model. Format overconstrain: loosen the output specification and describe the goal rather than the format. Incorrect inference: add explicit reasoning steps, ask the model to show its work before giving a final answer.

This diagnostic approach is more productive than iterating on wording until something works. Understand why it failed before deciding what to change.

The Role of Examples

Few-shot prompting -- providing examples of the input-output behaviour you want -- is one of the most reliable techniques in prompt engineering, and one of the most underused. A single well-chosen example can communicate a pattern more effectively than three paragraphs of instructions.

The key to good examples is not quantity but diversity. One example that captures the core pattern is often enough. Two examples that look superficially similar but represent different edge cases are more valuable than five examples of the same type. And negative examples -- showing what output you do not want -- can be as useful as positive ones for clarifying the decision boundary.

Chain of Thought and Why It Matters

For any task involving multi-step reasoning -- analysis, calculation, planning, diagnosis -- asking the model to reason through the steps before giving a final answer consistently improves output quality. This is not a trick. It works because it forces the model to allocate tokens to the reasoning process, making the intermediate steps available for the final answer to build on.

The practical implementation is simple: add "Think through this step by step before giving your final answer" or "Show your reasoning" to your prompt. For complex tasks, specify the steps explicitly: "First identify the key issue. Then list three possible causes. Then evaluate each cause against the available evidence. Then give your conclusion." Structure the reasoning process, do not just ask for it. That is where the skill actually lives.
